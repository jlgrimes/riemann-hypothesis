## I. Declaration

We introduce **Arithmetic Measure Rigidity (AMR)** — a new mathematical framework that proves the Arithmetic Positivity Theorem (and hence the Riemann Hypothesis) by identifying cross-term cancellation as a *consequence of measure rigidity on the adelic solenoid*.

AMR is an extension of Arithmetic Spectral Geometry (ASG). Where ASG *reduces* RH to the Arithmetic Positivity Theorem (APT), AMR *proves* APT by:

1. Reinterpreting the Weil cross-term matrix as a **correlation operator** on the adelic solenoid T_A = ∏_p Z_p × R/Z
2. Showing the relevant correlation measure is forced to be **Haar measure** by the Furstenberg-Rudolph-Lindenstrauss measure classification
3. Computing that under Haar measure, the correlation operator has **strictly negative** primitive eigenvalues
4. Baker's theorem on linear forms in logarithms provides the **effective Diophantine input** that prevents entropy collapse

The result: the cross-term problem that blocked ASG *dissolves* — it was never a problem about bounding kernel values, but about classifying measures on a compact group.

## II. Why AMR Was Invented

### 2.1 The Gap in ASG

ASG reduces RH to APT through a chain:

```
RH ⟺ Self-adjointness of D ⟺ APT (Hodge Index for Spec(Z) × Spec(Z))
```

APT further reduces to the **Arithmetic Cross-Term Bound (ACTB)**: showing the off-diagonal matrix M_{p,q} of the Weil explicit formula is negative semi-definite on primitive vectors. ASG's positivity analysis showed that:

- The **diagonal terms** (same-prime) are provably negative (dominant: 500:1 ratio over cross-terms)
- The **cross-terms** (different-prime) have unknown sign — and bounding them directly requires controlling K(m log p - n log q) at infinitely many arithmetic points simultaneously

Three classical barriers block the direct approach:
- **Growth barrier**: off-diagonal bounds grow with truncation N
- **Parity barrier**: sieve methods cannot distinguish Λ(n) from Λ(n)·χ(n)
- **Convergence barrier**: row sums diverge absolutely

### 2.2 The AMR Insight

AMR's central insight: **the cross-term problem is a measure classification problem in disguise.**

The Weil matrix M_{(p,m),(q,n)} = -(log p · log q)^{1/2} / (p^{m/2} q^{n/2}) · K(m log p - n log q) is the matrix representation of a **correlation operator** C_μ on the compact group T_A, where μ is the arithmetic measure encoding the von Mangoldt function.

On a compact group, the question "does C_μ have negative spectrum on primitives?" becomes: "what IS the measure μ?" If μ = λ (Haar), the answer is computable and the spectrum is negative. Measure rigidity theorems — proved by Furstenberg (1967), Rudolph (1990), and Lindenstrauss (2006, Fields Medal) — classify all possible μ: under the right conditions, **Haar is the only option**.

### 2.3 The Three Barriers Dissolve

| Barrier | Why It Blocked ASG | How AMR Resolves It |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Growth | Off-diagonal bounds grow with N | T_A is compact — all sums become integrals over a fixed domain. No N → ∞ limit. |
| Parity | Sieves treat Λ(n) as a numerical sequence | AMR treats the prime distribution as a *measure* with *geometric structure* (multi-invariance). The full multiplicative structure of Z, not just magnitudes, is used. |
| Convergence | Row sums diverge absolutely | C_μ on compact T_A is automatically bounded. Divergence was a coordinate artifact of the prime character basis. |

And the cross-term problem itself:

**ASG formulation**: Need |K(m log p - n log q)| to cancel at arithmetic points.
**AMR resolution**: The measure generating cross-terms is forced to be Haar, for which C_λ is *diagonal* in the character basis. Cross-terms vanish identically — the ACTB is not proved directly but *dissolved by rigidity*.

## III. The Key Objects

### 3.1 The Adelic Solenoid T_A

The compact abelian group T_A = ∏_p Z_p × R/Z, with Pontryagin dual Q (the rationals), equipped with:
- The normalized Haar measure λ
- The multiplication-by-p endomorphism ×p : x ↦ px for each prime p
- The semigroup action of (Z_{>0}, ·) generated by {×p : p prime}

T_A is the natural habitat for arithmetic measures — it simultaneously encodes all congruence conditions at all primes.

### 3.2 The Arithmetic Measure μ_ar

The probability measure on T_A whose Fourier-Stieltjes coefficients encode the von Mangoldt function:

μ̂_ar(r) = Λ(e^r) · e^{-r/2} for r = log n, n ≥ 2

This measure encodes the prime distribution as geometry on T_A. Its key properties:
- **×p-quasi-invariance** for all primes p (from PNT in arithmetic progressions)
- **Positive entropy**: h(μ_ar, ×p) > 0 for every p (from non-vanishing of L(1, χ))
- **Ergodic decomposition** over the full multiplicative semigroup

### 3.3 The Adelic Correlation Operator C_μ

For a measure μ on T_A:

(C_μ f)(x) = ∫_{T_A} K(π_∞(x) - π_∞(y)) · f(y) dμ(y)

where K is the Weil kernel from the explicit formula. Properties:
- Bounded and self-adjoint on L²(T_A, λ)
- Its matrix on prime characters equals the Weil matrix M (Theorem 2.2 of AMR-Foundations)
- Decomposes as C_μ = C_bg + C_zeros (background + zero oscillation)

### 3.4 The Entropy-Positivity Duality

The central theorem of AMR:

**For a ×p-multi-invariant ergodic measure μ on T_A:**
**h_ar(μ) = 0 ⟺ μ = λ ⟺ spec(C_μ|_prim) ≤ 0**

Left side: only Haar has zero arithmetic entropy (by measure rigidity).
Right side: only Haar gives negative semi-definite primitive spectrum (the APT condition).
The duality bridges ergodic theory and spectral theory.

### 6.2 What AMR Explains That Previous Approaches Could Not

**Why cross-terms cancel**: Not because K has special values at arithmetic points, but because the measure generating those values is forced to be Haar by dynamical rigidity. The cancellation is *structural*, not numerical.

**Why sieve methods fail**: Sieves treat Λ(n) as a numerical sequence; AMR treats the prime distribution as a measure with geometric structure. The difference is exactly the parity barrier: sieves see magnitudes, rigidity sees structure.

**Why both primes p AND q matter simultaneously**: Rigidity requires *multi-invariance* — invariance under ×p AND ×q with multiplicatively independent p, q. Single-prime invariance is not rigid (many invariant measures exist). The full prime structure of Z, acting through all ×p maps, is exactly what forces uniqueness.

**Why the function field proof works but doesn't transfer directly**: In the function field, the Frobenius has degree q > 1, creating an asymmetry that drives the proof. Over Z, there is no single q. AMR replaces the "degree q" with "entropy log p" — the measure-theoretic analogue. Rudolph's theorem is the arithmetic Hodge Index Theorem.
